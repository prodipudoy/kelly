Inter-generational trauma

Is psychological trauma that has transferred between families. Children, grandchildren and the future generation of first-generation survivors of Cambodian genocide and Vietnam war have inherited the residual trauma and developed their own mental health issues.

As this trauma is not limited to these horrific and terrifying events, it has actually been passed down to the next generations, either through subconscious behaviour or unethical mannerisms that has been accepted for a long time. Mental health in the Asian community is not talked about in everyday conversations because it is not visible to the eye, it is a taboo, leaving inter-generational Asians disconnecting from their feelings.

Inter-generational trauma is an ongoing issue and has significantly affected the mental health of children, grandchildren and future generation of first-generation survivors.

First generation survivors had very little time and space to heal from their trauma because they had to escape from danger to live a better life. The tragedy leaves painful wounds that have been deep-seated into their mind, almost like a permanent scar that cannot be removed. First generation survivors had a dream, and it was to project their wishes of good health, prosperity and planning a future for their children and grandchildren they never had.

Anxiety is described as having feelings of uneasiness and heightened worry. Anxiety can create chaos in the mind and sometimes overwhelm our five senses with dread, fear or panic. Whenever we are being pressured, threatened or feeling vulnerable, anxiety is a completely normal response to stress.

Often our body and mind will automatically respond to a challenging situation – our heart rate increases, breathing accelerates, and our sympathetic nervous system releases a surge of stress hormones. These hormones are known as the ‘fight, freeze or flight’ response, anxiety is a red flag that something is wrong. Once the stressful situation has passed, our physical symptoms of anxiety usually settles.

DEPRESSION

We can all feel low sometimes, often when we respond to different stressors. When we feel a bit numb, disconnected and disengaged, our sense of self, purpose and joy in life can be clouded from our low mood. Therefore, it is important we find alternate paths to move forward. Regardless of how depression makes us feel, we are not alone.

Depression can cause chaos with our sense of self, our motivation, and our relationships with others. Sometimes depression can weigh heavy on us as if we feel lost and cannot find joy in our life especially when there is no specific reason to feel this way. Some mental and emotional signs of depression can be feeling stressed and overwhelmed, losing interest in everyday activities, struggling with decision making, increasing sense of being irritable and frequently thinking of repeated negative thoughts. Some physical and behavioural signs of depression can be insomnia, constantly feeling tired despite having adequate sleep, changing in eating habits and loss of appetite, increasing use of alcohol, nicotine and other drugs and socially withdrawing from others.

When there is no intervention and depression is left alone, depression does not go away. For many people, depression can be very difficult to live with and can lost for weeks, months and sometimes years which can be detrimental to our overall physical, emotional and mental health and wellbeing.

Stress is a normal physical response to experiences that make us feel threatened. When our  body senses danger, the body’s natural defence automatically kicks into fight or flight response.  This reaction is our body’s way of protecting us from real or imagined life threatening situations.

How our body reacts and responds to different situations, can actually be beneficial for us. On one hand, it can allow us to be motivated and determined to run from danger or be effective at a task we need to complete like finishing an assignment or winning the grand final in a sports match. Stress can also benefit the immune system as it helps prepare the body to endure conditions like surgery and infections.

While stress can be useful to our day to day activities, it is important to be aware when our stress levels become unmanageable, it can lead to serious health problems where it can compromise and affect our health, mood, productivity, relationships and overall wellbeing. Stress can raise blood pressure, increase the risk of heart attack and stroke, contribute to infertility and suppress the immune system. We can also be at risk to being exposed to anxiety and depression.
